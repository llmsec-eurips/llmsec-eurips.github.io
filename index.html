Foundations of Language Model Security

Theory, Practice, and Open Problems

About

The field of language model security stands at a pivotal moment. Despite extensive research into adversarial attacks and defenses, the underlying principles governing these phenomena are poorly understood. Critical gaps persist in our knowledge of the mathematical and computational properties that give rise to vulnerabilities, the internal mechanics of how models process adversarial data, and the fidelity of our evaluation methods to real-world security scenarios.

To address this, our workshop will unite experts from the fields of adversarial robustness, conversational and sociotechnical AI safety, and broader LLM security. The primary objective is to investigate the fundamental mechanisms of language model vulnerabilities and to delineate a clear roadmap toward developing genuinely secure architectures.

Our approach is distinguished by its emphasis on first principles over incremental advances. Rather than cataloging novel exploits and patches, we pose foundational questions: What are the inherent mathematical properties that render language models susceptible to attack? How can insights from interpretability inform a more robust understanding of both attack vectors and potential defense strategies?

Our Novel Format

To increase knowledge transfer and make the most of in-person collaboration, we propose a new workshop format consisting of four thematic blocks. Each block includes an expert keynote, contributed talks, and extended guided discussion among participants, presenters, and domain experts.

Guided Discussion

Our format prioritizes guided discussion over talk density, with expert-moderated deep dives followed by short contributed spotlights. This design differentiates us from prior security workshops centered on enumerating attacks/defenses via a series of back-to-back talks.

Four Thematic Blocks

Block 1: Emergent Security in Complex AI Ecosystems

What new vulnerabilities arise in tool-using, memory-equipped, and centrally orchestrated agents?

Keynote: Verena Rieser

Block 2: Benchmarking LLM Security

Fundamental limitations of existing LLM security evaluations and future best practices.

Keynote: Joaquin Vanschoren

Block 3: Critical Pitfalls of Existing Work

What is missing from existing work? How do we secure the entire AI supply chain?

Keynote: Ilia Shumailov

Block 4: Evaluating Attack and Defense Efficacy

More practical threat models from an industrial perspective and AI security best practices.

Keynote: Kathrin Grosse

Call for Papers

Important: We accept talk proposals only—no papers and no archival proceedings. Each proposal should include a title, a 200-300 word abstract, and an optional one-page non-archival summary or single slide. Talks based on NeurIPS papers are ineligible to ensure fresh perspectives.

Important Dates

Paper Submission: October 10, 2025
Accept/Reject Notification: October 31, 2025
Workshop: December 6-7, 2025

Submission Guidelines

We plan to accept 8 short spotlight talks (7 minutes each, two per thematic block). Submissions will be managed via OpenReview with reviews by three randomly selected authors from other blocks. Selection emphasizes fit to foundational themes, clarity, novelty of insight, and potential to generate discussion. We explicitly encourage work-in-progress and preliminary findings that advance foundational understanding.

Topics of Interest

  • Mathematical foundations of adversarial robustness in language models
  • Interpretability-driven security analysis
  • Novel evaluation frameworks for real-world security
  • Computational properties underlying vulnerabilities
  • Secure-by-design architectures
  • Sociotechnical aspects of LLM security
  • Cross-domain transfer of security principles
